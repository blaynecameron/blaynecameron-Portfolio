    Overview

    ValueMine is an app created to simplify the investment process. Its purpose is to help value investors to determine the intrinsic value of companies by providing them with financial data.

    Inception

    The idea for the app came to me when I was learning Objective-C. I was attempting to retrieve the stock price of companies from Yahoo’s API, when I started to wonder if there were any apps that displayed in depth financial information for value investors. As someone who casually invests, this peaked my interest.

    Target Market

    Value investing requires a significant amount of research and data in order to make informed investment decisions. Value investors use a combination of year-to-date financials, historical financial information, financial ratios and information from SEC filings (i.e. 10-k) when investing. Most serious value investors already have their own methods for acquiring the information that they need, but these people are not our target market. We are targeting those who we call casual value investors. We define them as people who have full time jobs, but trade on the side. Casual value investors generally have to go to great lengths in order to acquire and organize the information they need to make investments.

    Research

    Most finance apps in the market only provide information about stock price trends, news, etc. But, these apps only cater to investors who make short term trades, like technical analysts, who trade based on stock price. After looking at several apps in the app store, I didnt find any that provided the financial information necessary for people who trade using long-term strategies such as value investors.

    Value investors can look at up to 10 years of financial information and much of this data is very tedious to get and organize. The primary way to get this information is either through the use of APIs, which generally cost money or by downloading SEC files. These methods aren’t very complicated, but the pain for investors comes from having to organize this information into spreadsheets or paying for or learning to use APIs.

    A casual value investor who I interviewed manually inputs financial data into a spreadsheet from SEC filings. A snapshot of a part of the spreadsheet can be seen below. This process, as you can imagine is very tedious and could take up to 2 hours to fill out 10 years of financial data for a company.

    The Intelligent Investor

    While, doing research I also discovered a very popular book on value investing called the “The Intelligent Investor” by Benjamin Graham. Graham was the mentor of Warren Buffett, who holds this book in very high esteem. In order to get a better understanding about the investment process, I turned to this book to gain insight about investment practices and to see how investors make decisions.

    Process

    With the information I acquired from interviews and Ben Graham’s book I created several user stories to help organize and prioritize features which could be included in the app. The user stories were placed in order of importance which helped us to decide which features were necessary for the MVP (Minimum Viable Product). We wanted to follow the MVP model as much as possible, and that meant stripping down the app to its core functions and identifying features that would be essential for our users. So, we had to ask ourselves what is the most important thing for value investors and why? The answer is simple, it’s data. More specifically, a company’s current and historical financial information. Investors look at current financial information because they need to be able to see the current state of a company and historical data is important because the past is a good predictor of the future when it comes to investing. With our new focus established, we decided that a sign in and search filter were only features which would be nice to have opposed to something critical for the app to do it’s job and they could be added in a later.

    Making The App More Intuitive

    I wanted the app to follow a similar layout to existing finance apps that are currently in the market. This would ensure that the app would be more intuitive for any first time user as they would be able to open the app and have an immediate understanding of how it works. By allowing users to have a watchlist they would be able to save their favourite companies and quickly have access to their data. A search view was a given feature seeing as users would need a way to find companies they are looking for and then a screen for user’s to view all the information for a company.

    Name

    Before choosing ValueMine as the apps name, there were several other names we explored. One was “snowball”, which was inspired by Warren Buffet’s book, “The Snowball Effect”. Another was “Pato”, which means duck in Spanish. The inspiration of this name came from the rubber ducks that are handed out at each meeting at Berkshire Hathaway.

    Both Pato and snowball were meaningful names, but they are not unique. If you search for either in the App Store you will see a lot of results and it can be very difficult for anyone who is trying to find your app. With ValueMine, we do not have this issue because it is easy for users to search for and find it. The name ValueMine was actually inspired by my friend who I developed the app with. While we worked on the app, he would always refer to it as “going into the mines”. The app is in a sense a mine. It is a mine of financial information that can be valuable.

    Branding

    People who invest place a lot of emphasis on the accuracy of numbers and information in order to make investment decisions. Therefore, ValueMine needed to convey a sense of trust and reliability through it’s logo and brand.

    The act of mining is normally associated with mountains and resources such as precious metals. But, when mining these mountains for resources, it is not like the resources are lying around everywhere in abundance. Rather, miners have to dig through a lot of dirt in order to uncover the resources they seek. Investing is a lot like this. Investors generally have to look through many companies before they are able to find even one that meets their investment criteria. The logo is abstract and represents this struggle. The two triangles represent mountains and they overlap to form a smaller triangle which is the value one can expect to find.

    Using bright colours with an app like this can potentially take a user’s attention away from what’s really important; the information. Because of this I chose to use neutral colours which would be very easy on the eyes.

    The Watchlist

    Watchlists are common features in finance apps. They allow users to look at companies they own shares in or that they’d like to own shares in. The watchlist in ValueMine serves a similar purpose with a slight twist. We created it with the idea of also allowing users to be able to quickly view the value of a company. So, aside from price we also displayed the P/E and P/B ratio which allow investors to see whether or not the company is potentially undervalued. But, having P/E, P/B and price in the same table caused the view to become cluttered.

    Refining The Watchlist

    To simplify the way the information was displayed on the watchlist I chose to allow users to choose whether they wanted to display price, P/E or P/B at any given time. To do this I included a segmented control in the design which would allow users to choose which data type they would like to display.

    Displaying Company Financials

    Generally, investors will look at financial data in a spreadsheet. Doing this is fine on a desktop because it has a large screen, but viewing spreadsheets on your smartphone can quickly become frustrating. The challenge for me was to figure out the best way to present all of this information without just using a spreadsheet. To make things even more challenging, I wanted all of the data to be accessible from one screen to reduce the amount of navigation a user had to make and provide a better user experience.

    In early wireframes I experimented with having a tab bar at the top of the screen in order to accomodate all of the information and separate the different data types into manageable groups. Each screen had its own purpose, one providing a summary, one to display year-to-date financials and one designated to show historical financial information.

    When In Doubt, Redesign

    After doing the early wireframes for the company financial view, I wasn’t satisfied. Deep down I knew that there was a better solution, so I started started over. But, I didn’t throw out all of my ideas I kept the important ones such as the tab bar which helped break up the information into manageable groups. I knew that this would be a good way to display all the necessary informaiton in one screen and reduce navigation.

    The company view was changed significantly in the final design. For this design I focused as much on separating the different types of information as I did on making the design appealing.

    Health Check

    Financial ratios are of tremendous importance to value investors. They can indicate how healthy a company is. In the early wireframes the summary was given it’s own screen due to the amount of information that was displayed making it only visible if the user chose to see it. We decided that it would be better to display this information at all times. To accomodate the additional information we separated it into two views managed by a page controller.

    Reusing Earlier Ideas

    The idea for separating the year-to-date and historical financials was kept, but the form of it was changed. I decided it would be better to use Apple’s built in segmented control to manage this action because it was already being used in the watchlist and having the same controls would complement the design of the app.

    The ten year financials in the end was set to be displayed on a seaprate view because of the amount of information thatis being displayed. The follow button was also changed to a plus sign and included on each company screen so that users would be able to follow companies regardless of what aspect of the company's financials they were looking at.
